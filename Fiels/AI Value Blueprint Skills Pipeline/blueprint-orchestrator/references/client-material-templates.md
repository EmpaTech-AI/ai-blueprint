# Client Material Templates and Data Requirements

**AI Assist BG — AI Value Blueprint Pipeline**
Reference document for consultants and client onboarding.

---

## Purpose

This document exists to do two things simultaneously: help clients provide the right materials in the right format, and help the AI consultant assess material quality before starting the Blueprint pipeline.

The quality of the output Blueprint is directly proportional to the quality of the inputs. A client who provides a complete, well-structured financial summary and a real org chart will receive a Blueprint with specific, actionable AI opportunity recommendations and accurate ROI estimates. A client who provides a marketing brochure and verbal descriptions will receive a Blueprint that requires significantly more remediation, assumption flagging, and consultant follow-up before it reaches delivery standard.

Use this document as the operational bridge between intake and pipeline launch. Share relevant sections with clients before they submit materials. Use the checklist section when materials arrive. If the incoming materials are marginal or insufficient, do not start the pipeline — address the gap first.

---

## How to Use This Document

- **During client onboarding:** Share the relevant category sections with the client as part of the pre-engagement brief. Not all 8 categories apply equally to every client — use judgment.
- **When materials arrive:** Use the Material Quality Quick-Assessment Checklist (see end of document) to evaluate what was received before triggering the pipeline.
- **Before pipeline launch:** Reference this document during the pre-pipeline material sufficiency assessment to determine whether the submission is Sufficient, Marginal, or Insufficient.
- **During pipeline execution:** When the AI consultant flags a data gap or assumption, reference the relevant category section to determine whether a workaround applies or whether client follow-up is required.

## The 8 Document Categories

---

### 1. Financial Summary

**Status: REQUIRED**

#### What This Is
A structured view of the client's revenue, costs, and key financial performance indicators. This is the foundational input for all ROI modeling, cost-of-inefficiency calculations, and prioritization logic in the Blueprint.

#### What We Need
- Total revenue (annual, ideally last 12 months)
- Revenue breakdown by business line, product, or service category
- Cost structure: major cost categories and approximate amounts (labor, technology, operations, marketing, etc.)
- Gross margin or contribution margin if available
- Key financial KPIs the business already tracks (e.g., cost per acquisition, revenue per employee, EBITDA margin)
- Any notable trends: growth rate, seasonality, declining lines

#### Ideal Format
A Profit & Loss statement for the last 12 months with line-item detail, exported from their accounting system or prepared by their finance team. An Excel or PDF export from QuickBooks, Xero, or similar is ideal. A management accounts pack works. A board-level financial summary also works if it contains actual numbers rather than projections.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
- Annual revenue range (e.g., "between €2M and €3M")
- The 3-5 biggest cost categories with approximate relative size (e.g., "60% labor, 15% technology, 10% rent")
- Whether the business is growing, flat, or declining

This minimum allows basic ROI framing but will produce ranges rather than specific figures. All financial outputs in the Blueprint must be flagged as estimates.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Actual P&L with line items, not summaries
- Revenue broken down by product/service line so we can identify where AI can move the needle
- Cost data that shows labor costs by function (not just "salaries" as a single line)
- Historical context: 2-3 years of trend data, even at high level
- The client has already identified which numbers they care about and why

#### Red Flags
- Only a bank statement (shows cash, not business performance)
- A marketing brochure or pitch deck with revenue claims but no actual financials
- Figures that are clearly aspirational ("we're projecting €5M this year") with no actuals
- Refusing to share financials at all — if this is the case, escalate before proceeding
- Numbers that contradict the intake form answers (e.g., intake says "50 employees" but labor costs suggest 5)

#### If You Don't Have This
If a client cannot or will not share financials, use the intake form financial indicators section (Section 3) to construct a proxy financial model. Flag all financial outputs in the Blueprint with a "Estimated — financial data not provided" marker. Increase the confidence score penalty accordingly per the Quality Gates framework.

### 2. Org Chart or Team Structure

**Status: REQUIRED**

#### What This Is
A structured representation of the client's departments, reporting lines, headcount, and key roles. Used to map AI opportunities to specific functions, estimate labor cost impact, and understand decision-making authority for implementation planning.

#### What We Need
- All departments or business functions
- Headcount per department (approximate is fine)
- Reporting structure (who reports to whom at the leadership level)
- Key roles that are central to operations or that appear frequently in AI use cases (e.g., operations lead, finance manager, sales director)
- Any roles that are outsourced, contracted, or shared services

#### Ideal Format
An actual org chart document — PowerPoint, PDF, Lucidchart export, or similar visual. An HR system export showing employee count by department and job title is also excellent. A spreadsheet with columns for Department / Role / Headcount / Reports To is highly workable.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
- A list of all departments with approximate headcount
- The name and role of the person responsible for each department
- Total headcount

This minimum is sufficient for function-level opportunity mapping but will not support detailed role-level impact analysis.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Actual org chart with names, titles, and reporting lines
- Headcount broken down by role type (e.g., 12 customer service agents, 3 supervisors, 1 director)
- Notes on which roles are high-turnover, hard to fill, or bottlenecks
- Indication of which roles involve the most repetitive or process-heavy work
- Context on recent org changes or planned restructuring

#### Red Flags
- Only the CEO's LinkedIn profile
- A marketing "About Us" page with headshots but no structure
- Org information that contradicts intake form headcount answers
- A chart that shows only the top 2 levels with no department-level detail

#### If You Don't Have This
Ask the client to complete a simple Department + Headcount table during onboarding. This takes 15 minutes and is non-negotiable for a REQUIRED category. A verbal org description captured in the intake interview is acceptable as a fallback but must be documented in the consultant notes and flagged in the Blueprint.

### 3. Sales or Pipeline Data

**Status: REQUIRED**

#### What This Is
A description or data export showing how the client acquires customers, manages deals, and converts prospects to revenue. Central to identifying AI opportunities in sales operations, lead qualification, proposal generation, and pipeline management.

#### What We Need
- Sales process overview: stages from lead to close
- Pipeline stages used in their CRM or tracking system
- Conversion rates at each stage (even approximate)
- Average deal size or revenue per customer
- Sales cycle length (average time from first contact to close)
- Volume: how many leads/opportunities per month
- CRM system in use (Salesforce, HubSpot, spreadsheet, etc.)
- Current sales team size and structure

#### Ideal Format
A CRM export or sales dashboard screenshot showing pipeline volume, stage conversion rates, and deal values. A sales performance report from HubSpot, Salesforce, or Pipedrive is ideal. A well-structured spreadsheet tracking deals by stage also works.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
- Written description of the sales process (can be prose)
- Approximate monthly lead volume and deal close rate
- Typical deal size range
- Name of CRM or tracking system used

This minimum supports qualitative opportunity identification but not quantitative pipeline impact modeling.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- CRM export with actual stage-by-stage conversion data
- Historical win/loss data with reasons for loss
- Data segmented by channel, product, or rep so we can identify where AI has the highest leverage
- Notes on where the sales team spends the most time (manual work, data entry, follow-up chasing)
- Information on what happens after close: onboarding, handoff, account management

#### Red Flags
- Only a marketing deck with revenue projections and no actual pipeline data
- Claiming there is no sales process (acceptable for some business models, but must be explained)
- CRM data that has clearly not been maintained (e.g., all deals in one stage, no dates)
- Sales figures that are dramatically inconsistent with the financial summary

#### If You Don't Have This
If there is no CRM and no documented sales process, this itself is a significant finding. Use the intake form to capture a verbal description of the sales process and flag it as a data gap. The Blueprint should include a recommendation to implement basic pipeline tracking as a prerequisite for sales AI deployment.

### 4. Process Documentation

**Status: REQUIRED**

#### What This Is
Documentation of how the business actually operates day-to-day — the workflows, standard procedures, and repeatable activities that AI could potentially automate, augment, or optimize. This is often the richest source of AI opportunity identification.

#### What We Need
- Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) for key business processes
- Workflow diagrams or process maps
- Documentation of recurring tasks: what triggers them, who does them, how long they take, what systems are involved
- Known bottlenecks, pain points, or error-prone steps
- Any processes that are currently manual but feel like they should be automated
- Information about how exceptions and edge cases are handled

#### Ideal Format
Written SOPs in Word or PDF format. Workflow diagrams from Lucidchart, Miro, Visio, or similar tools. Process documentation from business management systems (Notion, Confluence, Process Street). A well-organized set of process descriptions, even if informal, is highly workable.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
Written descriptions of 3-5 core business processes, each covering:
- What triggers the process
- Who is involved
- The main steps in sequence
- What systems or tools are used
- Where time is lost or errors occur

Even rough, informal descriptions are useful. The key is specificity — "we process invoices" is not useful; "we receive invoices by email, manually enter them into QuickBooks, match them to POs in a separate spreadsheet, then get approval from the finance director before payment" is excellent.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Documented SOPs with step-level detail and decision points
- Process maps that show handoffs between people or systems
- Time estimates attached to steps (e.g., "this step takes 20-30 minutes per invoice")
- Volume data (e.g., "we process 150 invoices per month")
- Honest identification of where things go wrong
- Processes that span multiple departments or systems — these are prime AI territory

#### Red Flags
- "We don't have documented processes" stated as a reason not to provide anything — this phrasing is actually useful data and should be noted, not dismissed
- Generic descriptions that describe what happens but not how ("we handle customer queries professionally")
- Process documentation that is clearly outdated and no longer reflects how the business operates
- Only documenting the easy, non-problematic processes while avoiding the messy ones

#### If You Don't Have This
The absence of documented processes is itself a significant finding for the Blueprint. If a client cannot provide process documentation, conduct a structured process elicitation exercise during the intake interview using the process discovery prompts in the intake toolkit. Document what you capture and flag it as consultant-reconstructed rather than client-provided. The Blueprint should note undocumented processes as a risk factor for AI implementation.

### 5. Marketing or Customer Data

**Status: RECOMMENDED**

#### What This Is
Data about who the client's customers are, how they are acquired, and how they are retained. Used to identify AI opportunities in marketing automation, customer segmentation, personalization, and retention.

#### What We Need
- Customer segments: who are the different types of customers
- Acquisition channels: how do customers find and choose the business
- Channel performance: which channels drive the most revenue vs. the most volume
- Customer retention metrics: churn rate, repeat purchase rate, customer lifetime value
- NPS or CSAT scores if tracked
- Customer communication tools in use (email platform, CRM, support system)
- Any existing customer data infrastructure (CDP, analytics platform, data warehouse)

#### Ideal Format
Marketing dashboard export from HubSpot, Google Analytics, or similar. A customer segmentation analysis or CRM report showing customer distribution by segment, revenue, and acquisition channel. An annual marketing review document works well. Even a well-prepared summary slide from a recent business review is useful.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
- Description of the main customer segments (2-5 types)
- List of acquisition channels in rough order of importance
- Whether customer retention is tracked and what the approximate retention rate is
- Name of any marketing or analytics tools in use

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Quantified customer segments with revenue contribution per segment
- Channel-level cost and revenue data (cost per acquisition by channel)
- Cohort analysis or LTV data
- Clear articulation of where the marketing team spends the most time
- Existing automation in place (shows AI readiness) alongside gaps where automation is missing
- Voice of customer data: survey responses, support ticket themes, reviews

#### Red Flags
- Only social media follower counts and engagement rates — this is vanity data, not business data
- Marketing data that exists but the client doesn't know what it means
- No customer data at all despite a large customer base (suggests poor data infrastructure — note this)
- Data from a single channel presented as representative of the whole business

#### If You Don't Have This
Use the intake form customer context section to capture a qualitative description of customer segments and channels. Flag marketing and customer AI opportunities as lower-confidence in the Blueprint. Note the absence of customer data infrastructure as a potential prerequisite investment in the roadmap.

### 6. Technology Inventory

**Status: RECOMMENDED**

#### What This Is
A comprehensive list of the software, systems, and tools the business currently uses, including how they connect to each other. Essential for assessing AI integration feasibility, identifying data sources, and avoiding recommendations that conflict with the existing tech stack.

#### What We Need
- All software and SaaS tools in use: name, vendor, primary purpose
- Systems that contain important business data (CRM, ERP, accounting, HRIS, etc.)
- Integration landscape: which systems connect to which, and how (API, manual export, native integration)
- Data flows: where does business data originate and where does it end up
- IT infrastructure overview: cloud vs. on-premise, key hosting providers
- Approximate number of users per system
- Systems that are under contract review or planned for replacement

#### Ideal Format
A technology inventory spreadsheet with columns for: System Name / Vendor / Purpose / Users / Key Integrations / Data Stored / Contract Status. An IT asset register. A systems architecture diagram. Even a well-organized list in a document is workable.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
- A list of all main software systems with a one-line description of their purpose
- Which systems contain the most important business data
- Whether systems are integrated with each other or operated independently

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Full inventory including shadow IT and departmental tools, not just IT-approved systems
- Integration map showing data flows between systems
- Identification of systems where data quality is poor or where manual workarounds exist
- Notes on upcoming tech changes (migrations, new implementations, contract endings)
- API availability or documentation for key systems
- Notes on which systems the team finds frustrating or limiting

#### Red Flags
- "We use standard tools" with no specifics — this is not useful
- A list that only covers one department while ignoring others
- Significant contradictions with other submitted documents (e.g., intake mentions a CRM but no CRM appears in the tech inventory)
- Evidence of significant technical debt or a highly fragmented, unintegrated stack presented without acknowledgment

#### If You Don't Have This
Use the intake form technology section and any systems mentioned in other submitted materials to construct a proxy tech inventory. Conduct a brief systems elicitation during the intake interview using the standard technology discovery prompts. Flag AI integration recommendations as subject to technical validation in the Blueprint.

### 7. Strategic Documents

**Status: OPTIONAL**

#### What This Is
High-level planning documents that describe where the business is going: board decks, annual plans, strategic roadmaps, investor presentations, or management team offsites output. Used to ensure the Blueprint's AI recommendations align with strategic direction and to surface strategic priorities that AI can accelerate.

#### What We Need
- Current strategic priorities (12-month and 3-year horizon if available)
- Key initiatives underway or planned
- Growth targets and the assumptions behind them
- Areas the leadership team has identified as needing improvement
- Any strategic risks the business is managing
- M&A activity, geographic expansion, or significant business model changes in scope

#### Ideal Format
A recent board deck or strategic plan document. An annual planning presentation. An investor update with strategic narrative. A management team planning offsite summary. Even a well-articulated strategic memo works.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
A summary of the top 3-5 strategic priorities for the next 12 months, either as a document or captured during the intake interview.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- A board deck from the last 6 months with strategic narrative and financial targets
- A roadmap showing initiative sequencing and ownership
- Explicit identification of where the business feels constrained or at risk
- Evidence that leadership has already been thinking about AI or digital transformation
- Strategic context that helps explain apparent contradictions in other submitted materials

#### Red Flags
- A strategy document that is more than 2 years old and has clearly not been updated
- Strategy that is entirely aspirational with no operational grounding
- Documents that are for external audiences only (investor relations) with no internal operational detail

#### If You Don't Have This
Rely on intake form Section 2 (Strategic Context) as the primary source of strategic direction. If the intake form strategic section is also thin, note this as a risk: Blueprint recommendations may not align with unstated strategic priorities, and a leadership alignment session is recommended before roadmap finalization.

### 8. Previous AI or Digital Initiative Descriptions

**Status: OPTIONAL**

#### What This Is
A description of any AI, automation, or significant digital transformation initiatives the client has previously attempted or is currently running — what was tried, what the outcomes were, and what lessons were learned. Used to calibrate AI readiness, avoid repeating past failures, and build on what already works.

#### What We Need
- Names and brief descriptions of past or current AI/automation projects
- Which department or function each initiative touched
- What the intended outcome was
- What actually happened: success, partial success, failure, abandoned
- Why initiatives succeeded or failed (technology, adoption, resourcing, wrong problem, etc.)
- What is currently running in production
- Vendor relationships related to AI or automation tools
- Internal capability built: does the team have AI/data literacy?

#### Ideal Format
Project briefs, post-mortems, or lessons-learned documents from past initiatives. A summary slide from a technology review. Even a well-prepared verbal description captured during the intake interview is highly valuable.

#### Minimum Viable Submission
A brief description (even a few sentences) of each initiative that was tried, what happened, and the main reason for the outcome.

#### What Makes This Excellent
- Honest post-mortems that explain what went wrong, not just what was attempted
- Evidence of successful automation that can be extended
- Identification of specific vendors or tools that did or did not work for this organization
- A team that has learned from past failures and can articulate what they would do differently
- Current initiatives with clear owners, metrics, and timelines

#### Red Flags
- Claims of extensive AI deployment that do not appear in the technology inventory
- Initiatives described in exclusively positive terms with no challenges noted
- Evidence of repeated failed attempts at the same problem without learning

#### If You Don't Have This
The absence of prior AI or digital initiatives is itself useful and important data. It indicates that AI is genuinely new territory for this organization, which affects the Blueprint's recommended implementation approach: more foundational infrastructure work, more change management emphasis, longer timelines for initial initiatives, and lower initial complexity recommendations. This absence should be noted explicitly in the Blueprint's AI Readiness Assessment section rather than treated as a gap to work around.

**Quality Level Definitions:**
- **Rich** — Exceeds minimum. Contains specific data, quantified metrics, and useful context. Pipeline can run with confidence.
- **Adequate** — Meets minimum viable submission standard. Pipeline can run; some outputs will use ranges or estimates rather than specifics.
- **Thin** — Below minimum viable. Either the document is missing, contains wrong-category content, or lacks the core data needed. Pipeline should not start for affected workstreams until gap is addressed.

**Overall Submission Assessment:**

| Rating | Criteria |
|--------|----------|
| **Sufficient** | All 4 Required categories are Adequate or Rich. |
| **Marginal** | 3 of 4 Required categories are Adequate or Rich; 1 is Thin with a documented workaround available. |
| **Insufficient** | 2 or more Required categories are Thin, or any Required category has no workaround. Pipeline should not start. |
